Black Desert Online

Channel-Based Sharding Action MMORPG

A seamless open-world MMO built on Pearl Abyss's proprietary engine, featuring real-time action combat, a player-driven economy via the Central Market, and large-scale siege warfare that has historically pushed its server infrastructure to the breaking point.

Overview

Black Desert Online runs on a custom proprietary engine built from scratch by Pearl Abyss founder Kim Dae-il, who made the deliberate decision to build in-house rather than license an existing engine. The studio was founded in 2010, and the engine was developed alongside the game itself, optimized specifically for seamless open-world streaming and the fast rendering demands of large-scale PvP with hundreds of characters on screen.

The game uses a "One World" server model where each geographic region (NA, EU, KR, etc.) is a single logical world. Within that world, players can freely switch between multiple channels, which are parallel instances of the same persistent game world. Characters, inventories, and progression are shared across all channels in a region. This is distinct from traditional "server" selection seen in older MMOs, where characters were locked to a specific shard.

The engine prioritizes client-side prediction for its action combat system, which requires frame-precise inputs for dodging, combos, and grab moves. This design choice makes the combat feel responsive at the cost of introducing desync problems during large-scale PvP, where the gap between what the client predicts and what the server confirms becomes painfully visible.

Seamless Streaming

The world loads continuously as the player moves. No zone boundaries, no loading screens between regions. The engine uses spatial partitioning to stream terrain, objects, and NPCs in and out of memory based on proximity. Only instanced content like certain dungeons triggers a loading screen.

Action Combat Netcode

Combat uses client-side prediction with server reconciliation. Player inputs are processed locally for instant feedback, then verified by the server. The gap between client state and server state is the root cause of desync in PvP, especially at high player density or across high-latency connections.

Channel Architecture

Each region runs dozens of channels. Players pick a channel on login and can swap freely (with a short cooldown). Channel swaps are blocked during world boss spawns and near node war start times. Special "Siege" and "Node War" channels exist for scheduled PvP events.

Global Self-Publishing

Pearl Abyss originally licensed publishing to regional partners (Kakao Games for NA/EU, GameNet for Russia). By 2021, Pearl Abyss took over self-publishing worldwide, unifying the update pipeline and reducing the content gap between regions.

Server Infrastructure

Pearl Abyss operates BDO's servers across multiple geographic regions. The NA/EU infrastructure was historically hosted through Leaseweb, a provider that became a point of significant community criticism due to performance issues during peak load events like sieges. When Pearl Abyss took over self-publishing from Kakao Games in February 2021, they consolidated the publishing pipeline but the underlying hosting situation evolved only gradually.

The One World System

Each region (NA, EU, KR, JP, SEA, etc.) is a single logical "world." A player's family (account) exists across all channels within that region. Characters, warehouse storage, the Central Market, guild data, and contribution/energy points are all shared. You can switch channels freely with a 15-minute cooldown, and your character stays exactly where they were geographically.

Channel Types

  • Standard Channels (Balenos-1, Serendia-2, etc.): Normal PvE/PvP channels with karma penalties for open-world killing.
  • Season Channels: Restricted to seasonal characters with boosted progression, separate from main servers but sharing the same persistent world data.
  • Arsha (PvP) Channel: No karma penalties for killing other players. Popular for contested grind spots.
  • Node War / Siege Channels: Dedicated channels that host scheduled large-scale PvP. Non-participants may be kicked from these channels before war starts.
  • Olvia Channels: Boosted XP channels restricted to new or returning players.

Hosting and Provider Issues

The NA/EU servers ran on Leaseweb infrastructure during the Kakao Games publishing era. Community investigations found Leaseweb to be among the lowest-rated hosting providers for game servers. Despite thousands of players reporting lag, desync, and disconnections, Kakao initially blamed individual ISPs rather than acknowledging server-side problems. This was a persistent source of frustration, especially during large-scale PvP.

The Valencia Siege Incident (March 2018)

27 guilds placed forts in the Valencia region for what became the largest siege in BDO history across all regions. Over 1,400 players were present on a single battlefield. When the siege started, approximately 95% of participants were disconnected. For the next 90 minutes, gameplay consisted of logging in, walking a few meters, and getting disconnected again. This event became a rallying point for infrastructure complaints.

Network & Protocols

BDO's networking is built around the demands of its action combat system. The game uses a hybrid approach where the client handles input prediction and animation locally while the server performs authoritative state validation. This is fundamentally different from tab-target MMOs where the server can process everything at a leisurely pace.

Client-Side Prediction

When a player executes a combo or dodge, the client plays the animation immediately and sends the action to the server. The server validates the action, checks cooldowns and resource costs, and either confirms or rejects it. In most cases, the server confirms and the player never notices the round trip. In high-latency or high-load scenarios, the server may reject actions the client already showed, creating visible desync.

Desync in PvP

Desync has been the single most persistent technical complaint in BDO's history. In large-scale PvP, the gap between what your client shows and what the server actually computed becomes significant. Players routinely report being grabbed by enemies who appear to be facing the wrong direction, taking damage from attacks that visually missed, or having their own attacks fail to register despite showing hit effects on screen.

The root cause is the combination of fast-paced action combat (which demands client-side prediction for responsiveness) with high player density (which overwhelms server tick rates). Pearl Abyss has made incremental improvements over the years, including reducing unnecessary packet data and optimizing server-side processing, but the fundamental tension between responsiveness and accuracy persists.

Data & Persistence

BDO persists a staggering amount of data per player. Each "family" (account) contains multiple characters, each with their own gear, skill builds, and lifeskill levels. On top of that, the family shares contribution points, energy, warehouse storage across cities, worker empires, node investments, horse stables, fairy companions, and garden plots.

Central Market

The Central Market replaced the older territory-based Marketplace in a major architectural overhaul. The old system used a lottery-based bidding mechanism for high-value items: when something was listed above 3 million silver, it entered a bidding phase where the winner was selected randomly. This was territory-restricted, requiring players to be in the right city.

The new Central Market is a centralized order-book system. Players place buy orders at a chosen price, and when a seller lists at that price, orders are fulfilled in queue order. Prices float dynamically within a band (base price +/- 7.5%), and the base price itself shifts based on supply and demand. The market is accessible from anywhere via the ESC menu and even from the Black Desert+ mobile companion app while offline.

Worker Empire System

Workers are NPCs assigned to resource nodes. They run on server-side timers, cycling between nodes to gather materials and return them to city warehouses. Worker quality (white/green/blue/yellow) affects cycle time and yield. The entire worker empire runs as background server processing, consuming no client resources. Players manage hundreds of workers across dozens of nodes.

Node Investment

Contribution Points (a shared family resource) are invested into nodes on the world map to unlock resource gathering, establish trade routes, and (allegedly) boost item drop rates. Node connections form a graph structure that the server validates for trade route bonuses. Disconnected nodes break trade distance calculations.

Horse Breeding & Training

Horse data includes tier, level, skills learned, appearance, breeding count, and training progress. Horses level up through distance traveled (tracked server-side). Breeding combines two horses' genetics to produce offspring with probabilistic skill and tier inheritance. Auto-pathing distance is logged even during AFK play.

Lifeskill Processing

Processing, cooking, and alchemy run as server-validated sequences. The client sends the craft request, the server checks materials and mastery level, processes the recipe, and returns results. Mastery scores (gear-based) act as server-side multipliers on yield. AFK processing queues are maintained server-side until materials run out.

Scaling & Performance

BDO's scaling challenges center on two scenarios: normal open-world load (which the channel system handles adequately) and large-scale PvP events (which have historically broken everything).

Channel-Based Load Distribution

The channel system is BDO's primary horizontal scaling mechanism. When player counts rise, the load spreads across channels. Each channel is essentially a complete copy of the game world running on dedicated server resources. Players self-select their channel based on preference, and the game gently suggests less populated channels during login.

Node War & Siege Scaling Failures

Node Wars pit guilds against each other on specific channels at scheduled times. Guilds place forts, build structures, and fight to destroy each other's forts. The scale ranges from small skirmishes (20v20) to territory wars with hundreds of participants.

The server's ability to handle these fights has been a moving target. Pearl Abyss has capped the number of guilds that can place forts in a single region and introduced "Fortress Siege" mode, a faster 20-minute format that limits participant count. These are design-level workarounds for infrastructure limitations.

Technical Deep Dives

World Boss Cross-Channel Synchronization

World bosses operate on a fixed weekly schedule and share HP across all channels simultaneously. When Kzarka spawns, it spawns on every channel at the same time with a single shared health pool. Damage dealt on any channel reduces the same pool. When the boss dies, it dies on all channels. This is managed by a dedicated boss synchronization service that aggregates damage from all channel servers.

During boss spawns, two restrictions kick in: PvP is globally disabled for 15 minutes (controversial, since bosses only affect specific locations), and channel swapping is blocked to prevent players from hopping channels to claim loot multiple times. The PvP lockout was justified as reducing server load and preventing griefing at boss locations, but PvP-focused players objected to the blanket restriction affecting areas nowhere near the boss.

Seamless World Streaming

The Black Desert engine streams world data continuously as the player moves. The engine uses spatial partitioning to determine which terrain tiles, objects, and NPCs need to be in memory at any given time. As you ride a horse across the continent, the engine is constantly loading terrain ahead of you and unloading terrain behind you.

This streaming applies to the client only. The server always has the full world state in memory for its channel. The server does not need to stream, because it does not render. It tracks entity positions, validates actions, and processes background systems (workers, marketplace, boss HP) regardless of where players are.

Pearl Abyss evolved this technology into the BlackSpace engine for Crimson Desert, adding hierarchical level subdivision, hierarchical proxy loading, and imposter rendering for even more efficient distance streaming. These techniques prioritize rendering what is closest and replace distant geometry with lightweight imposters.

The Remastered Upgrade

In 2018, Pearl Abyss released the "Remastered" graphics mode, adding physically-based rendering (PBR), enhanced global illumination, and YEBIS post-processing effects. From a networking perspective, the remaster did not change the client-server protocol. The additional rendering load was entirely client-side, meaning players with weaker hardware could still run the original graphics mode while sharing servers with remastered players.

Ocean Content & Bartering

The ocean expansion added bartering as a lifeskill, where players sail between island NPCs exchanging goods in multi-step trade chains. The bartering inventory, exchange rates, and refresh timers are all server-side. The ocean also introduced large-scale sailing with guild ships and the Vell world boss (a sea boss requiring coordinated guild groups across ships). Ocean physics, including ship movement and wave interaction, are primarily client-side visual effects. Ship position is server-tracked but movement feels client-authoritative for responsiveness.

Global vs. Regional Publishing

BDO originally used a licensing model: Pearl Abyss developed the game in Korea and licensed publishing rights to regional partners. Kakao Games (originally Daum Games) published NA/EU, GameNet handled Russia, and other partners covered Japan and Southeast Asia. Each publisher ran its own servers, managed its own patch schedule, and could be weeks or months behind the Korean version.

Pearl Abyss had already self-published in Korea, Russia, and Japan before taking over NA/EU in February 2021. The transition required players to migrate their accounts from Kakao's platform to Pearl Abyss's platform before a June 2021 deadline; accounts that were not transferred were permanently deleted. The consolidation to self-publishing reduced the content gap between regions and gave Pearl Abyss direct control over server infrastructure decisions.

Pearl Abyss founded. Kim Dae-il begins developing the custom Black Desert engine from scratch rather than licensing an existing engine.

Korean closed beta. First public tests of the seamless open world and action combat systems on live servers.

Korean launch. Published by Daum Games (later Kakao). Channel system and node/siege war architecture go live.

NA/EU launch. Published by Kakao Games on Leaseweb infrastructure. Desync complaints begin immediately during large-scale PvP.

Remastered graphics update. PBR rendering, YEBIS post-processing added. Client-only change; no networking modifications. The Valencia mega-siege with 1,400 players exposes infrastructure limits.
2019
Central Market replaces old Marketplace. The bidding/lottery system is retired in favor of a centralized order-book economy with dynamic pricing and companion app access.
2019
Pearl Abyss acquires Korean publishing from Kakao. Begins consolidating global publishing rights, starting with Korea, Russia, and Japan.
2020
Ocean expansion and bartering system. Adds server-managed multi-step trade chains across ocean islands. Vell ocean boss introduced.
2021
Pearl Abyss takes over NA/EU publishing. Account migration deadline set. Kakao Games exits BDO publishing. Server infrastructure begins transition.
2023
Fortress Siege mode introduced. A shorter, more constrained PvP format designed to work within server performance limits. Ongoing packet optimization for node wars.
2025
BlackSpace engine announced for Crimson Desert. Next-generation evolution of the BDO engine with improved streaming, physics, and rendering. BDO continues on the original engine with incremental updates.
